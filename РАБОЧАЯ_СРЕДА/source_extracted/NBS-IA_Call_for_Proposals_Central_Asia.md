1. Background  
Infrastructure forms the backbone of economies by providing essential services such as 
transportation, energy, and water. However, it remains highly vulnerable to natural disasters as 
climate risks intensify. It is estimated that 14% of global GDP growth is at risk annually due to 
loss of infrastructure services from climate-related events, with populations in developing 
countries facing heightened vulnerability. The World Bank projects that between USD 57 and 95 
trillion in infrastructure will be built worldwide by 2030. To prevent locking in future vulnerabilities 
and to enhance the resilience of existing infrastructure, smarter policies, planning, design, and 
investments are imperative to boost the integration of Nature-based Solutions (NbS) 1  into 
infrastructure development. 
NbS offer vital responses to multiple climate-related challenges across urban and natural 
environments by mitigating the impacts of extreme heat, flooding, drought, landslides, soil 
erosion, and air pollution. Numerous studies conclude that NbS can often be more cost effective 
than grey infrastructure solutions in delivering services than grey infrastructure solutions, and 
provide additional economic, environmental, and social benefits. The examples include long-
term performance appreciation, potential economic savings from climate-related hazards, 
increased biodiversity and ecosystem resilience, and improved human well-being and quality of 
life. This holistic approach positions NbS as a strategic component of sustainable, resilient, and 
adaptive infrastructure development.  
In response to the urgent need to increase climate resilience and integrate NbS into existing and 
new infrastructure, the Italian Ministry of Environment and Energy Security (IMEES/MASE), in 
collaboration with the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) and the Organization for 
Economic Co-operation (OECD), launched the Nature-based Solutions Innovation Accelerator 
(NBS-IA). This 5-year initiative aims to accelerate the uptake of NbS approaches to enhance 
infrastructure resilience in both Central Asia and Africa. Through competitive calls for proposals, 
the initiative provides grant support to consortia that present innovative pilot projects that can 
demonstrate the highest climate adaptation impact and financial and operational viability.  
2. Regional Context in Central Asia   
Central Asia faces acute climate vulnerabilities due to its reliance on glacier-fed rivers and water-
scarce drylands, with accelerating warming, glacier retreat, and diminishing snowpack already 
cutting late-season flows essential for irrigation, hydropower, and livelihoods. The region is 
experiencing above-average global warming, driving elevation-dependent glacier loss in the Tian 
Shan and Pamirs mountains, reducing summer water availability and heightening risks of 
droughts, floods, and glacial-lake outburst floods2.  
Since these climate impacts are already disrupting water supplies, logistics networks, and 
energy infrastructure, the region urgently needs resilience-focused infrastructure to reduce risk 
and safeguard development gains. UNECE Environmental Performance Reviews, as well as their 
national climate and development frameworks including Nationally Determined Contributions 
(NDCs) and National Adaptation Plans (NAPs) and green economy strategies, reiterate the urgent

3. Objectives  
This Call for Proposals seeks to support the integration of NbS in grey infrastructure that 
demonstrate clear and measurable climate adaptation impacts, generate practical 
implementation evidence, and create pathways for scaling and replication across Central Asia. 
The overall objective of this Call is to enhance the climate resilience of critical infrastructure 
systems in Central Asia through the design and piloting of NbS in grey infrastructure that produce 
demonstrable adaptation benefits. Specifically, the Call is looking for pilot projects that can:  
✓ Reduce climate-related risks on infrastructure assets and services through NbS; 
✓ Demonstrate the technical, financial, and institutional feasibility of sustainable 
infrastructure through NbS approaches in the Central Asian context; 
✓ Generate measurable evidence, primarily on climate adaptation potential and other co-
benefits, for instance, climate mitigation potential, contribution to addressing societal 
challenges, protecting biodiversity, etc. and demonstrate cost-effectiveness; 
✓ Strengthen regional, national or local capacity for designing, piloting, and managing 
sustainable infrastructure through NbS; 
✓ Support measuring impact (compared to Business as Usual).  
✓ Identify replication and scalability potential.  
 
4. Scope and Typology 
4.1 Geographic Scope  
This Call is open to proposals implemented in Central Asian countries, including:  
Kazakhstan, Kyrgyz Republic, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, and Uzbekistan.  
4.2 Eligible Typologies  
For the purpose of this Call, only interventions that apply NbS to enhance the resilience of either 
existing or future “grey” infrastructure are eligible. Each proposal must explicitly identify the 
target grey asset, the relevant climate hazard, the causal mechanism by which the NbS reduces 
risk/exposure/vulnerability, the infrastructure service protected or improved, and the 
baseline/business-as-usual scenario.  
These types of interventions are infrastructure systems that combine natural or nature-based 
components with engineered or grey infrastructure to deliver infrastructure services and reduce 
risks. For example, riparian afforestation and wetlands can work together with built

This Call for Proposals can provide two types of grants: 1) Design Grant and 2) Pilot Grant. Under 
Pilot Grant, two types of interventions are eligible: Type A) NbS centred pilot interventions, and 
Type B) Hybrid NbS pilot interventions, as defined below. Proposals must be focused on 
protecting and enhancing infrastructure service delivery or improving the climate resilience of 
existing or planned grey infrastructure.  
Please note that standalone NbS projects that are not linked to grey infrastructure are NOT 
eligible. For example, rainwater harvesting projects, sustainable agriculture projects, 
ecosystem restoration projects that do not have any interaction with any grey infrastructure, in 
terms of protection, enhancement and/or operability. Pure research projects are also not eligible.   
Proposals submitted under the Design Grant window should focus on the design, feasibility and 
preparation activities of integrating potential NbS interventions into grey infrastructure. 
Identification of innovative solutions are encouraged, including concepts at early-stage 
development.  Design and preparation study must be linked to the preparation of a particular 
intervention on the ground. Pure research studies are not eligible.  
The maximum amount for Design Grant is US$300,000.  
Proposals submitted under the Pilot Grant window must include pilot interventions on the ground. 
Design and preparation studies could also be included with a budget up to US$ 300,000. For Pilot 
Grants, a minimum readiness threshold is required. Proposals must demonstrate, via a 
readiness matrix, that the site is identified, the asset owner/operator is involved, a preliminary 
hazard diagnosis is available, land/access status is clarified, environmental/social risks are 
preliminarily screened, an O&M entity is identified, and public authority engagement is 
evidenced.  
Type A: NbS centred pilot interventions  
NbS centred pilots are interventions in which the proposed solution relies exclusively on natural 
or ecosystem-based processes (e.g. vegetation, soils, wetlands, water bodies) to protect or 
enhance service delivery by grey infrastructure. These interventions do not include altering the 
original design or role of any grey infrastructure components.  
For example, native forest restoration in upstream mountain catchments supports downstream 
hydropower through landslide mitigation, while watershed and riverside restoration improve 
water supply, and vegetated roadside buffers control erosion and dust storms. Agroforestry 
corridors and green walls along energy transmission/distribution lines reduce flooding and soil 
erosion. Urban green corridors mitigate flood risks and urban heat-island effects.

The budget for Type A pilot grant is between US$ 500,000 – US$ 1,000,000 (including design and

Type B: Hybrid NbS pilot interventions 
Hybrid NbS pilots combine Nature-based Solutions with existing or planned engineered or grey 
infrastructure components. Nature-based elements are either physically integrated into grey 
infrastructure assets to enhance performance and climate resilience (e.g. riparian 
bioswales/vegetate buffers integrated to roads, tree shelterbelts integrated to canals/roads, 
green roofs and rain gardens integrated in urban buildings), or green and grey components 
function as distinct but coordinated elements that jointly reduce climate risk (e.g. riparian 
afforestation + river embankments).  
The budget for Type B pilot is between US$ 1,000,000 – US$ 1,500,000 (including design and 
preparation studies if needed). This type of proposals could include a budget of maximum of 
20%3 of the piloting grant amount (excluding design studies) on grey components4.

5-2-2. Potential for Scalability, Replicability, and Additionality (30%)  
Proposals should demonstrate the potential for scalability and replicability in similar contexts, 
as well as additionality. This includes:  
• 
Scalability: Clear pathways for expanding the solution to different sites and assets, 
beyond the implementing country and or region.  
• 
Replicability: Evidence that the solution can be adapted to other countries or regions 
with minimal modifications. 
• 
Additionality: Proposals must explicitly demonstrate and justify why the project requires 
NBS-IA grant support and would not proceed under business-as-usual conditions or 
existing financing mechanisms, demonstrating true additionality of UNEP's intervention. 
Applicants may specify which form of additionality applies: financial (funding gap), risk 
(grant absorbs initial risk), technical (supports demonstration/validation), institutional 
(enables coordination or permitting), market (unlocks a replicable but not yet bankable 
model), others or a combination.  
Proposals are encouraged to demonstrate synergies with existing or planned initiatives in the 
same location and/or sector by other institutions, including but not limited to government 
agencies, local organizations, multilateral development banks, and international organizations.
